ECE 598 — VLSI Chip Tapeout Lab

VLSI Compute-In-Memory ASIC

Jan 2026 – Apr 2026 Team Captain · 5 members TSMC 65LP
SystemVerilog Cadence Innovus Synopsys DC Scan Chain DFT TSMC 65LP
Overview

The VLSI Compute-In-Memory (CIM) ASIC project was completed as part of ECE 598, a newly developed Apple-sponsored VLSI tapeout lab at the University of Michigan, where I served as team captain for a five-member design team. Our project explored a fully digital CIM architecture aimed at addressing the growing memory bottleneck in modern AI workloads by performing computation directly within memory. We designed a latch-based CIM core supporting 1-bit weights and 4-bit activations, combining in-memory bit-serial computation with synthesized peripheral control and accumulation logic. The design followed a complete RTL-to-GDSII ASIC flow in TSMC 65LP using SystemVerilog, Synopsys Design Compiler, and Cadence Innovus. Through extensive verification (including randomized testbenches, block-level simulations, and post-synthesis and post-layout timing validation) we ensured full functional correctness across all stages. The final design successfully taped out with clean DRC/LVS signoff, achieving a 500 MHz operating frequency, 51,437.88 μm² core area, and 19.37 mW power consumption.

Key contributions
  • Served as team captain, leading a 5-member team through all stages of the ASIC design flow from architecture to tapeout
  • Directed the full RTL-to-GDSII flow for a CIM architecture, owning design, synthesis, and physical implementation
  • Led a mid-project architecture pivot from a hand-routed 6T SRAM core to a scalable latch-based RTL implementation
  • Led verification efforts, including randomized testbench development, waveform debugging in Verdi, and post-synthesis/post-layout validation
  • Managed cross-team integration, ensuring compatibility with shared chip infrastructure, including successful integration into a global scan chain

EECS 427 — VLSI Design I

16-bit RISC-V Processor

Sep – Dec 2025 Team of 5 TSMC 65nm
Cadence Virtuoso Cadence Innovus NCVerilog TSMC 65nm Hand-layout
Overview

The 16-bit RISC-V Processor Design Project was the course project for EECS 427 (VLSI Design I) during my Junior Fall semester (FA25) at the University of Michigan and served as my undergraduate major design experience. Our five-member team designed and implemented a custom 16-bit pipelined RISC processor in TSMC 65 nm technology, incorporating a complete baseline instruction set architecture, datapath, control logic, arithmetic units, and memory interface circuitry. The project combined full-custom circuit design with RTL development, synthesis, and automated place-and-route using an industry-standard ASIC flow. Through extensive NCVerilog verification, custom testbench development, pre/post-layout SPICE simulations, and full DRC/LVS signoff, we validated complete processor functionality and successfully executed a Fibonacci benchmark program. The final design achieved a 162 μm × 200 μm core area and operated at a maximum clock frequency of 200 MHz.

Key contributions
  • Served as a primary technical contributor across the full project lifecycle, helping drive architecture, implementation, and integration efforts within the team
  • Designed transistor-level schematics and hand-laid the 16-bit register file, ALU (ADD, AND, OR, and XOR), and logarithmic shifter
  • Developed RTL, performed synthesis, and completed automated place-and-route for the program counter unit with branch resolution logic and the instruction controller/decoder
  • Wrote custom NCVerilog testbenches for functional and corner-case validation
  • Performed pre-layout and post-layout SPICE simulations to verify timing and correctness with extracted parasitics
